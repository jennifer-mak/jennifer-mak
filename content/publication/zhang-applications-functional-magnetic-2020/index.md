abstract: Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) is a neuroimaging tool which
  has been applied extensively to explore the pathophysiological mechanisms of neurological
  disorders. Spatial neglect is considered to be the failure to attend or respond
  to stimuli on the side of the space or body opposite a cerebral lesion. In this
  review, we summarize and analyze fMRI studies focused specifically on spatial neglect.
  Evidence from fMRI studies have highlighted the role of dorsal and ventral attention
  networks in the pathophysiological mechanisms of spatial neglect, and also support
  the concept of interhemispheric rivalry as an explanatory model. fMRI studies have
  shown that several rehabilitation methods can induce activity changes in brain regions
  implicated in the control of spatial attention. Future investigations with large
  study cohorts and appropriate subgroup analyses should be conducted to confirm the
  possibility that fMRI might offer an objective standard for predicting spatial neglect
  and tracking the response of brain activity to clinical treatment, as well as provide
  biomarkers to guide rehabilitation for patients with SN.
