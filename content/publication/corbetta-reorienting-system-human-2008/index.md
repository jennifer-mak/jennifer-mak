abstract: Survival can depend on the ability to change a current course of action
  to respond to potentially advantageous or threatening stimuli. This \"reorienting\"
  response involves the coordinated action of a right hemisphere dominant ventral
  frontoparietal network that interrupts and resets ongoing activity and a dorsal
  frontoparietal network specialized for selecting and linking stimuli and responses.
  At rest, each network is distinct and internally correlated, but when attention
  is focused, the ventral network is suppressed to prevent reorienting to distracting
  events. These different patterns of recruitment may reflect inputs to the ventral
  attention network from the locus coeruleus/norepinephrine system. While originally
  conceptualized as a system for redirecting attention from one object to another,
  recent evidence suggests a more general role in switching between networks, which
  may explain recent evidence of its involvement in functions such as social cognition.
